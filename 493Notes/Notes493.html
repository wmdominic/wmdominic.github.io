Chapter 1: The Study of Grammar: An Overview

Terms and Definitions

Dialects
Shared linguistic features of a group of people, often one from a particular region or of a particular ethnic group or social background (p. 339)
Regionalisms
A characteristic feature of the pronunciation or structure of the language spoken in a particular region of the country (p. 347)

Notes

"There is no one correct or proper version of the English language: There are many. Even the version we call American English has a wide variety of dialects. Different parts of the country, different levels of education, different ethnic backgrounds, different settlement histories--these factors produce differences in language communities. Every variety of English is equally grammatical--equally systematic, complex, logical, and expressive" (p. 4).

Three definitions of Grammar:

  1. Grammar 1- The system of rules in our heads:
    • It's important to recognize that these internalized rules vary from one language community to another. (For example, plural "you." Some say 'you or you all' and others say 'y'all.)
  2. Grammar 2- The formal description of the rules:
    • The formal description of language, which identify in an objective way, the form and structure (the syntax) of sentences.
  3. Grammar 3- The conventions of common usage, sometimes called linguistic etiquette:
    • The "dos and don'ts" of usage. This applies to words seen as inappropriate in certain occasions or settings, as well as using incorrect or inappropriate grammar.

Terms and Definitions

Affix
Is an additional block that can be put on or in a word. (Prefixes, Infixes, and Suffixes)
Inflectional Affixes
Morphemes that are added to the open classes to change their grammatical rule in some way.
Morpheme
A morpheme is the smallest unit of meaning.
Noun
A word that can be made plural or possessive.
Open Classes
The types of 'words' that can be added to. (These are nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs.) Opposed to Closed Classes, which are types of words that we do not add to. (These are prepositions, conjunctions, pronouns, articles, and determiners.)
Phrase
A phrase is a group of words that functions as a unit in a sentence.
Noun Phrase
A noun phrase will always have a headword. A headword of a NP is always a noun. The headword is considered a simple subject. A NP will often contain a signaler, marker, or also called a determiner. See Chapter 7

"Other Components" are determined by what kind of verb we have.
Verb
A word that shows tense.
Transitive Verb
Requires at least one complement, the direct object, in order to be complete. (There can be more than one, but there must be no less than one.) Ex. Cats fight for territory in the alley. (This example has two complements.)
Intransitive Verb
Almost always does not require a complement. Ex. Cats fight. (This example has no complements.)

Chapter 3: Sentence Patterns

Terms and Definitions

Adjectival (ADJ)
Can also be considered the subject complement. Renames or modifies the subject.
Adverbial (ADV)
Adverbs that answer time or place. Adverbials modify the verb (AKA: verb modifiers).
be Verbs
The be verb forms we are using in Chapter 3 are: am, is, are, was, were. The others (like have been, was being, might be, and will be) will be discussed in Chapter 4.
Direct Object
Direct object has a referent different than the subject, as indicated with the subscript II. Think of the the subject as the doer and the object as the receiver. Although this doesn't always work, so you may want to think of the DO as the answer to a what? or whom? question.
For example: The students organized (what?) a fundraiser.
Indirect Object
An indirect object can be thought of as the recipient of the sentence. For example: I sang a song to my grandma. Grandma would be the indirect object.
Intransitive Verb
The intransitive verb pattern (number six) does not have a complement after the verb.
Linking Verb
Applies to verbs other than be completed by a subject complement.
Noun Phrase (NP)*
A headword with all its surrounding modifiers (simplified book definition). See Chapter 2's Definition for more.
NPI
NPI will always refer to the subject (or initial Noun Phrase).
NPII & NPIII
NPII & NPIII refer to the direct object and indirect object when respectable.
*The NP gains a new I when the noun phrase is referring to a new entity.
Subject Complement
A subject complement completes the verb and modifies or describes the subject. An adjectival or noun phrase that describes, characterizes, or identifies the subject.

Sentence Patterns (1-5)

Pattern I: NP + be + ADV
NP (subject) + be (predicating verb) + ADV (adverbial of time or place)
Ex: The students are upstairs.
The ADV will be in a prepositional form describing where or when, like "We are in the swimming pool on Friday." (Here we actually have both time and place in one sentence.)
Pattern II: NP + be + ADJ
NP (subject) + be (predicating verb) + ADJ (subject complement)
Ex: The Internet connection was decent.
The ADJ here acts as a subject complement.
Pattern III: NPI + be + NPI
NPI (subject) + be (predicating verb) + NPI (subject complement)
Ex: The kid is a troublemaker.
The multiple NPIs in Pattern III indicate that the two noun phrases have the same referent. The be verb acts like an equal sign.
Pattern IV: NP + V-lnk + ADJ
NP (subject) + linking verb + ADJ (subject complement)
Ex: The soup tastes salty.
Pattern IV is a common category for verbs of the senses (i.e. taste, smell, feel, sound, look, as well as grew and seem).
The way to recognize linking verbs is to understand the role of the subject complement, to recognize the form of the structure following the verb and its relation to the subject.
Pattern V: NPI + V-link + NPI
NPI (subject) + linking verb + NPI (subject complement)
Ex: The kid became a troublemaker.
Very few linking verbs will fit this pattern, as most of them take adjectivals. Become and remain are the most common linking verbs found for Pattern V.

Optional Slots

The optional slot is an adverbial slot that can appear in every sentence pattern. Every sentence has its requirements to be a complete sentence, but most sentences often include information beyond that.

Some examples of each pattern with the optional ADV slot:

  • Pattern I: I was in the library at 2:30 pm.
  • Pattern II: Yesterday, the COVID risk meter was yellow.
  • Pattern III: Paul is a magician on the weekends.
  • Pattern IV: The band always sounds good.
  • Pattern V: The book remains a classic with the students in the CAL program.

You get the point. We can add an optional ADV slot just about anywhere in any pattern, and we can add as many as we want. We can also do this with Pattern VI through Pattern X (39).

Sentence Patterns Continued (6-10)

Pattern VI: NP + V-int
NP (subject) + Intransitive Verb
Ex: Sylvia slept on the bus.
Intransitive verbs do not have a compliment following the verb.
Pattern VII: NPI + V-tr + NPII
NPI (subject) + Transitive Verb + NPII (direct object)
Ex: The quarterback threw the football.
When there is a reflexive pronoun (ex. myself) or a reciprocal pronoun (ex. each other), the direct object actually will have the same NPI as the subject,
instead of NPII because the subject and object refer to the same thing. This falls under this pattern because how the verb works here.
Pattern VIII: NPI + V-tr + NPII + NPIII
NPI (subject) + Transitive Verb + NPII (indirect object) + NPIII (direct object)
*I will be underlining the indirect object.
Ex: Brenda's father bought her a car.
The direct object and indirect object slots can be switched as well:
NPI (subject) + Transitive Verb + NPII (direct object) + NPIII (indirect object)
Ex: Jeremy gave his homework to the professor.
Pattern IX: NPI + V-tr + NPII + ADJ
NPI (subject) + Transitive Verb + NPII (direct object) + ADJ (object complement)
Ex: The employees consider the job difficult.
Verbs commonly found in this pattern are prefer, like, consider, make, and find.
Pattern X: NPI + V-tr + NPII + NPII
NPI (subject) + Transitive Verb + NPII (direct object) + NPII (object complement)
Ex: The babysitter considers the child a pain.
The possibility of inserting the words to be between the direct object and the following slot can serve as a test for Patterns IX and X. If to be is possible, then what follows is an object complement.

Sentence Types

Declarative Sentences
The purpose of Declarative Sentences is to state, or declare, a fact or opinion.
I walked to the store.
Interrogative Sentences
The purpose of Interrogative Sentences is to ask questions.
Did Martha walk to school?
Imperative Sentences
The purpose of Imperative Sentences is to give commands.
Please, do the dishes.
Exclamative Sentences
The purpose of Exclamative Sentences is to express strong feelings.
I won the lottery!

In this case, 'laughed' and 'eaten,' are past participles, and 'laughing' and 'eating' are present participles.

Examples of participle phrases, which is a participle together with all of its complements and modifiers (345):

Kicking the ball, Bobby made a goal.


The Irregular Be
This is the only English verb with more than five forms. Be actually has eight forms.
Infinitive form: to be
Present tense: am, is, are
Past tense: was, were
-en form: been
-ing form: being

Auxiliary Verbs

(Helping Verbs)

Auxiliary System Rules/Formulas
* ( ) = Optional
  1. The base form is used both by itself and with should and may.
  2. The -ed and -s forms are used only by themselves, never with an auxiliary word.
  3. An -en form is used after a form of have (have and had). (have + -en)
  4. The -ing form is used after a form of be (be, am, were, and been). (be + -ing)
  5. The main verb (MV) is always the last word in the string. MV
    • (have + -en) (be + -ing) MV
  6. Besides have and be, the sentences illustrate another kind of auxiliary--will, should, may, and could--modal auxiliaries (M)
  7. When a modal is selected, it is always first in line.
    • (M) (have + -en) (be + -ing) MV
  8. The final piece is tense, which is added to the front. T
    • T (M) (have + -en) (be + -ing) MV

This is the Verb-Expansion Rule: T (M) (have + -en) (be + -ing) MV

Modal Auxiliaries

Present
will, shall, can, may
Past
would, should, could, might
Special Modals
must and ought to do not have past forms.

Using the Verb Forms

Base form and -s Form (Simple Present)
Uses: Historical, Habitual, Propensity (natural tendency), or Timeless Present.
Examples: I run every Tuesday. Lunch begins at 2:30. She sings in the choir. The boys play basketball.
Pres + be + -ing + MV (Present Progressive)
Uses: Present action of limited duration.
Examples: He is cooking dinner. Henry is enjoying the birthday cake.
Past + MV (Simple Past)
Uses: Specific point in time.
Examples: I danced at the Prom in 2013. I wrote that paper last year.
Past + be + -ing + MV (Past Progressive)
Uses: Past action of limited duration (often to show one particular action during a larger span of time).
Examples: The teacher lectured during the whole class time this morning.
Pres + have + -en + MV (Present Perfect)
Uses: A completed action extending from a point in the past to either the present or near present--and action with relevance to the present--or occurring at an unspecified past time.
Examples: The students' performance has improved this year.
Past + have + -en + MV (Past Perfect)
Uses: Past action complete before another action in the past.
Examples: The driver had used up all the gas in his gas tank.
Pres + have + -en + be + -ing + MV (Present Perfect Progressive)
Uses: Past action continuing into the present.
Examples: Egyptians have been irrigating the Nile River for centuries.
Past + have + -en + be + -ing + MV (Past Perfect Progressive)
Uses: Continuing past action completed before another action in the past.
Examples: The band had been playing songs for the audience before the power went out across town.

Subjunctive Mood

Mood
refers to the distinctive form of verbs.
Indicative mood
Used for statements of fact or opinion.
Imperative mood
Used for commands and directions.
Subjunctive mood
Used to signal necessity, desire, unreality, or conjecture. Look for that or if statements.
Ex: The teacher insists that students be on time to class.

Chapter 5: Changing Sentence Focus

Passive Voice

There are three indicators to determine a sentence is passive:
  1. An object, usually the direct object, becomes the subject.
  2. A form of be is added as an auxiliary; it teams with the past participle, the -en form of the verb. In other words, we add be + -en to the active verb string.
  3. The original agent (subject), if mentioned, becomes the object of the preposition by. This third step is often missing; it is not required. Most passive sentences do not include the active agent.
Ex (active): Ms. Franklin created the test.
Ex (passive): The test was created by Ms. Franklin.
Changing Passive Voice to Active
There are three steps that "undo" the three indicators above that make a sentence passive:
  1. Identify the agent or true subject or "doer" of the verbal action. If the agent is named, you'll usually find it in a by prepositional phrase. If a by prepositional phrase or agent, just add "someone."
    • A surprise party is being set up by his wife. [Agent: his wife]
    • The homework should be finished by Tuesday. [Agent: someone]
  2. Next, delete be + -en from the verb string.
    • is being set up = pres + be + ing + be + -en + set up
    • needs to be finished = past + shall + be + -en + finish
  3. Rewrite the sentence with the agent in the subject position, the revised verb in place, and the passive subject shifted to the direct object slot.
    • His wife is setting up a surprise party.
    • Someone should finish the homework by Tuesday.

Passive Voice or Active Voice?

Passive voice is oftentimes seen as negative. This is due to passive sentences sometimes having a vague or unnatural quality, which can usually be improved by being changed to active voice. Though in most cases, this is because of a problem elsewhere in the sentence, not necessarily passive voice itself. The issue could lie in the fact that the focus of the sentence is off, which can cause the sentence to be in poor quality.

"The main job of passive voice is to provide cohesion. The passive voice enables the writer to shift emphasis in the sentence so that the reader will put the focus where it should be—on the new information" (93).

Passive voice is also used when the "identity of the agent is obscured in history or simply has no bearing on the discussion" (93).

Passive voice is common and encouraged in technical and scientific writing, legal documents, and lab reports, where the researcher is the agent. For example, "I added 10ml water to the solution every 10 minutes" is active but unnecessarily includes the researcher, so "Ten milliliters Water was added to the solution every 10 minutes" would be more appropriate but is passive.

Some cases of passive voice just are more direct than active. For example, "Joe was sent to Iraq" (94).

Passive voice is used in "official-ese" or "bureaucrat-ese" to disguise or avoid the agent all together so the reader or audience does not know who is responsible. For example:

"It was noted in today's expense reports that there will be budget cuts and some employees will be terminated. Severance packages have been established for those being let go. There are options to find a new position within the company."

In this example, there is no agent mentioned. We don't know who "noted, cutting the budget, terminating employees, or established." This is often used when someone does not want to mention or reveal "who" was responsible for something.

The There Transformation

The there transformation provides the writer a way of changing sentence focus by altering the word order by opening the sentence with the unstressed there, known as an expletive.

The there transformation applies when the subject of the sentence is indefinite: "a book" or "an apple" rather than "the book" or "the apple."
The indefinite article is the signal that the subject of the there transformation is new information; we use the definite article for old or known information.

For example:

There's a book on vegan baking on the shelf.
When reading this sentence out loud, there is no stress on the "there" but rather there's stress on "book,"" as it is the focal point of the sentence.
There's the book on vegan baking.
This time, because the specific book is known, the stress is on "there" this time rather than "book".

The expletive form of there is always unstressed.

The adverb form of there, when opening a sentence, is nearly always stressed. This one acts like a pointer. "It's THAT specific thing."

Cleft Sentences

Cleft Sentences
Allow writers to accomplish by means of word order what a speaker can do by varying the point of the main stress or loudness.
SHARON gave a speech on Civil Rights on Friday. (It wasn't Jacob.)
Sharon gave a SPEECH on Civil Rights on Friday. (It wasn't a slideshow.)
Sharon gave a speech on CIVIL RIGHTS on Friday. (It wasn't on the Holocaust.)
Sharon gave a speech on Civil Right on FRIDAY. (Not on Thursday.)
Each of these show the stress that can be placed throughout the same sentence while speaking, though writing does not call for all-caps like this. This is where two types of clefts come in:
It-cleft
With it-clefts, you'll notice that the phrase following was is stressed, like the all-caps in the previous set of sentences do.
It was Sharon who gave a speech on Civil Rights on Friday.
It was a speech that Sharon gave on Friday.
It was Civil Rights that Sharon gave a speech on, on Friday.
It was Friday Sharon gave a speech on Civil Rights.
What-Cleft
With what-clefts, a what clause is put in the subject position, and was separates the original sentence into two parts.
What Sharon gave was a speech.
Sometimes what shifts the original verb phrase into subject position. Again, a form of be is added as the main verb.
What scared me most as a child was the dark.

Chapter 6

Modifiers of the Verb: Adverbials

The Moveable Adverbials

In the following sentence, there are three underlines structures–an adverb, a prepositional phrase, and a clause–each of which add adverbial information to the verb sang:

The choir boy sang angelically within the church sanctuary when the organ began to play.

The choir boy sang (How?) angelically.

The choir boy sang (Where?) within the church sanctuary.

The choir boy sang (When?) when the organ began to play.

Even though all the adverbials in the prior sentence follow the verb, there is no fixed spot for most adverbials, which is why moveability is one of the most telling characteristics of Adverbials. For example:

When the organ began to play, the choir boy sang angelically within the church sanctuary.

Within the church sanctuary, the choir boy sang angelically when the organ began to play.

"The position may depend on the writer's emphasis, the rhythm of the sentence, its relation to the previous sentence, or simply on the desire for sentence variety" (109).

Adverbs

Adverbs of Manner
-ly words, such as lovely, angrily, sadly, and usually tell "how" or in "what manner." For example: She spoke softly = in a soft manner
Manner adverbs such as these are the most moveable adverbials, as they can appear before or after the verb or at the beginning or end of the sentence.
Slowly, the old lady ate her oatmeal.
The old lady slowly ate her oatmeal.
The old lady ate slowly.
The old lady ate her oatmeal slowly.
A single-word adverb can even come within the verb strong, between and auxiliary word and the main verb:
The car was immediately turned over to the police as evidence.
Or between auxiliaries:
I have suddenly been stuck with anxiety.
Furthermore, manner verbs can be marked by qualifiers, words such as very, quite, so, rather.
I very softly walked through the house.
And like the adjectives they come from, these adverbs can be made comparative or superlative by adding more or most.
Dr. Carollo reads his poetry out load most articulately.

Single-word Adverbs
Besides the -ly adverbs, many other single-word adverbs provide information of time, place, frequency, and the like, including, but not limited to: now, then, nowadays, today, often, always, sometimes, seldom, never, here, there, everywhere.
You seldom do laundry during the week.
Never have I seen her here.
Some of these can be compared or qualified.
I ate too fast.
Quite often, I oversleep on weekends.

Prepositional Phrases

Prepositional phrases are one of the most common modifying structures, appearing regularly as noun and verb modifiers.

Common prepositions include: above, across, against, along, among, around, at, before, behind, below, beneath, beside, between, by, down, from, in, into, near, of, off, on, to, toward, under, upon, with and within.

Some prepositional phrases have modifiers that qualify or intensify them:

James sat down quickly in his desk.

Mr. White cried softly into his pillow.

Sentences often have more than one adverbial prepositional phrase:

Santiago ran to his mother's house in the rain on Sunday morning.

Like adverbs, adverbial prepositional phrases are rather moveable, but those referring to time tend to be more moveable than those referring to place, especially when found in the same sentence.

Around 9 AM on Tuesday, the building caught on fire in the city.

In the city, the building caught on fire on Tuesday around 9 AM.

Prepositional phrases can modify nouns as well as verbs, which can cause or ambiguity. For example:

He is going to eat the the spaghetti on the floor. This can be understood as:

He is going to eat the spaghetti that has fallen on the floor/eating it off the floor

He is going to eat his spaghetti dinner while sitting on the floor.

Nouns and Noun Phrases

"Nouns and noun phrases that function adverbially from a fairly short list designating time, place, manner, and quantity" (114). For example:

He went south. (place)

I ate breakfast this morning. (time)

Paul lifted 100 pounds at the gym. (quantity)

She flew first-class. (manner)

Verb Phrases

"The most common form in an adverbial role is the infinitive" (117). This is the same as an infinitive phrase.

Molly drove her car to bring her sister to school.

Wilson paid to provide his little brother lunch.

As an added trick to see if the infinitive is working as a an adverbial verb phrase, replace the "to" in the infinitive phrase to "in order to." If this works, the infinitive is acting as an adverbial infinitive phrase.

Molly drove her car in order to bring her sister to school.

Wilson paid in order to provide his little brother lunch.

Dangling infinitives occur when the subject of the sentence is no longer the subject of the adverbial infinitive phrase or is absent.

To keep the employees happy, good incentives are needed.

This sentence does not have uncertain or unclear meaning, and the reader will not have difficulty understanding. But there is a sense of ambiguity that can be cleared up with the addition of the subject.

A manager need good incentives to keep employees happy.

Sometimes participles and participle phrases can have an adverbial function, providing information of time, place, reason, and manner, as other adverbials do:

The couple fought screaming at each other.

Clauses

All sentences and the patterns listed in Chapter 3 are considered clause, but not all clauses are sentences!

Dependent Clause (Subordinate Clause)
Any clause that is not a complete sentence on its own.
Independent Clause (Main Clause)
Any clause that is a complete sentence on its own.
When the train came to a halt, the tourists stumbled.
"When the train came to a halt" is a dependent clause as it is an adverbial phrase.
"The tourists stumbled" is an independent clause because it is a complete sentence on its own.
Complex Sentence
Any sentence that includes a dependent clause of any kind.
Because there's a chance for rain, I packed rain boots and a jacket.
"Because there's a chance of rain" is a dependent clause as it begins with the subordinating conjunction, because.'
"I packed rain boots and a jacket" is an independent clause.
Compound Sentence
A sentence with two or more independent clauses.
Mark really enjoyed dinner; he ate everything on his plate.
Both "Mark really enjoyed dinner" and "he ate everything on his plate," are independent clauses and can stand alone, but since they are connected with a semi-colon, this because a compound sentence.
Compound-Complex Sentence
A sentence with a dependent clause and more than one independent clause.
Since it was cold outside, the dog slept next to the fireplace, and the fire crackled through the night.
"Since it was cold outside" is a dependent clause because it starts with a subordinating conjunction.
"The dog slept next to the fireplace" and "the fire crackled through the night" are both independent clauses.

Punctuation of Adverbials

Some opening adverbials are set off by commas while some are not. The use of these commas tend to be stylistic choice, especially when it comes to phrases. Generally a short prepositional phrase or noun phrase or an adverb will not be set off:

Quickly they all sprinted to the storm shelter.

Around 7AM the birds began to chirp.

With longer prepositional phrases, there is a choice:

On sunday morning after church the family goes to brunch.

At the elementary school during the flood, volunteers made lunches for the displaced and homeless.

When the end of the adverbial slot is not readily apparent, the comma will help any misunderstanding:

In the summer, nights come with a cool breeze.

Verb phrases and clauses are always set off by commas:

To become a teacher, I had to read many books for class and independently.

To rollerskate, you have to have great balance.

When the moon is out, wolves begin to howl.

When an adverbial interrupts the verb phrase for special effective, it will be set off by commas:

The telemarketer, annoyingly, called me twice during dinner.

When the opening phrase is parenthetical, or clearly a comment on the whole sentence than a typical adverbial, then a comma is requested:

"According to all known laws of aviation, there is no way a bee should be able to fly" (The Bee Movie, 2007).

Chapter 7

Modifiers of the Noun: Adjectivals

Headword
The word signaled by a determiner. This is a noun that is the main subject of the sentence. If a Noun Phrase is only one word, the headword is Noun Phrase. The following are examples of noun phrases and some of what can be added to them to modify the headword/noun. These words that modify the headword/noun are called Nominals.
She is sick. (She is the noun phrase and the headword.)
The gnome sits next to the tulips. (The gnome is the noun phrase; The is a determiner; gnome is the headword.)
The really old woman needed help across the street. (The really old woman is the noun phrase; The is a determiner; really old is an adverb and adjective modifying the noun/headphrase; woman is the headword.)
The photographs of her great-grandparents' wedding were discolored with time. (The photographs of her great-grandparents' wedding is the noun phrase; the is a determiner; photographs is the headword; of her great-grandparents' wedding is a adjectival prepositional phrase.)
Randy's new Instagram account has photos of his vacation to Hawaii. (Randy's new Instagram account is the noun phrase; Randy's is a determiner; new is an adjective (noun modifier); Instagram is a noun modifier; account is the headword and subject of the sentence that's being modified by 'new' and 'Instagram'.)

The Determiner

Determiner
A determiner is the word class that signals nouns. This category includes articles, possessive nouns, possessive determiners, demonstrative determiners, and numbers.
When you see one of these kinds of words, you can almost guarantee that it is beginning a noun phrase.
Some nouns, though, are not used with determiners, such as proper nouns (George, Nancy), non-countable nouns (atoms, sand), abstract nouns (freedom, peace), and sometimes plural count nouns (bananas, kids).
This pencil is yellow.
All her cats sleep in the sun.
Those ducks are mean.

Adjectives and Nouns

When there are both an adjective and a noun modifying within a noun phrase, they must appear in that order (adjective then noun).

We say, "Craig's new pool table is green," never, "Craig's pool new table is green."

Adjectives as Noun Modifiers
The adjective slot of a sentence or noun phrase frequently has more than one adjective, all of which will modify the headword.
My smooth old bright green plastic ball rolled into the street. (All of the underlines words describe and modify ball.)
When there are more than one adjective next to each other, putting a comma in between them depends on the context. If the two adjectives can be separated by and, a comma should be added.
The beautiful pink rose. (You wouldn't naturally say, "A beautiful and pink rose," so a comma is not necessary.)
The busy, lively bees swarmed the patch of daisies. (Here we can say, "The busy and lively bees swarmed the patch of daisies," so a comma is necessary.)
Generally, when two adjectives are of the same class, they require a comma to separate them. "Beautiful" and "pink" are not similar adjectives, as one is abstract and one is a color. Whereas, "busy" and "lively" are describing physical behavior.
The adjective can be qualified or intensified.
the dangerously yummy piece of pie a seriously strong smell of coffee
Prenoun Participles
Participles can also be used as an adjective.
The speeding car ran the red light.
Because participles are verbs, they are also commonly modified by adverbs.
Her loudly crying child could not be consoled.
Sometimes we create a compound adjective by hyphenating to make it clear that the adverbial modifies the prenoun participle.
(The hyphen rule for this occasion is that the -ly (slowly, peacefully, etc.) do not take hyphens; other adverbs (well, fast, etc.) do take hyphens. (133)
a long-lasting flavor
Hyphens are also needed when the first modifier applies to the second modifier and not the headword.
My part-time job at the gas station. Fargo had record-breaking snowfall last night.
Hyphens are also needed when a complete phrase is used in the adjective slot.
That's just an off-the-cuff idea.
The book suggests that hyphens are not needed if the adjective phrase is at the end of the sentence unless its idiomatic "possibly". (133-4)
The back-to-back interviews went well. The interview are back to back.
Her middle-of-the-road performance got 7.8 out of 10 from the judges. She got 7.8 out of 10 from the judges, but her performance was middle-of-the-road.

Prepositional Phrases

Adjectival Prepositional Phrases
Modifies a noun and is formulated identically to adverbial prepositional phrases, like in Chapter 6.
These adjectival prepositional phrases describe the noun headword in relation to time, place, direction, purpose, origin, etc.
The relatively dim lightbulb on the bedside lamp looks like it's on its last legs.
I have always wanted that house on the corner of Elm Street.
Adjectival prepositional phrases assist in identifying a noun or pronoun by answering the questions: "Which one?" or "What kind of?"

Relative Clauses

Relative Clause / Dependent Clause
The relative clause identifies the noun or pronoun it modifies and typically appears directly after that noun or pronoun.
The school that was remodeled last summer is holding a book fair next week.
A relative clause, with its subject-predicate structure, is a sentence pattern. The only difference is the noun is now a relative pronoun (who, whose, whom, which, or that).
Three features of a relative pronoun that will signify a relative clause:
  1. The relative pronoun renames the headword of the noun phrase in which it appears. (In the example above, "that" refers to "school.")
  2. The relative pronoun fills a sentence slot in its own clause. (In the example above, "that" is in the subject slot.)
  3. The relative pronoun introduces the clause, no matter what the slot it fills.
All relative pronouns fill slots in clauses that nouns normally fill. Though, some adjectival classes are introduced by relative adverbs where, when, and why.
Where clauses modify the verb within its own clause, but really is adjectival towards the noun that precedes it. For example:
That's Sanjay's house where the party was last week.
When clauses modify nouns of time.
I remember when grandma would tell me stories when we would have dinner together.
Why clauses modify the noun reason.
I can't tell you the reason why they screamed and left the room.

Participle Phrases

Participle Phrases
In the previous section with relative clauses, we could have a phrase like: the kitten that plays with the yarn
We can change this by adding be + -ing: the kitten that is playing with the yarn
This phrase can be reduced to: the kitten playing with the yarn This phrase, which is more or less a reduced relative/dependent clause, is an adjectival participle phrase.
This noun phrase, with its participle phrase as a modifier, will fit any nominal slot.
My favorite is the kitten playing with the yarn. (direct object)
The kitten playing with the yarn is rambunctious. (subject)
I like to play with the kitten playing with the yarn. (object of the preposition)
Three key features that will assist with participle phrases:
The previous example is a present participle, as it uses the -ing form of the verb in the participle phrase.As well, the present participle phrases are active because of the -ing form.
  1. Verbs from all four classes (be, linking, intransitive, transitive) can function as participles.
  2. The noun phrases in all NP slots can include participles or participle phrases as modifiers.
  3. The noun that the participle modifies is its subject. In other words, the relationship between the headword of the noun of the noun phrase in which the participle is embedded and the participle itself is a subject-verb relationship.
Passive Participles
Another common form of the adjectival participle is in the -en form. This form, normally called the past participle, is more accurately called the passive participle.
The portraits painted by my grandpa in high school are in a local museum.
The apples being eaten by the kids are extremely red.
Like the -ing participles, the -en participles are also reduced clauses.
The portraits that were painted by my grandpa in high school are in a local museum.
The apples that are being eaten by the kids are extremely red.
Refer to Chapter 5 for passive and active sentences and what makes a sentence or verb passive.
Moveable Participles
Although we often see the participle or participle phrase after the headnoun (in the postheadnoun slot), participles can be moved to the beginning of the sentence when modifying
Running the assembly, the principal struggled keeping the students' attention.
Born in 1922, Betty White is known as the Queen of Television.
The participle phrase that modifies the subject can also be shifted to the end of the sentence.
The shelf had way too many books on it, causing it to fall over.
The boat swayed in the water, riding the waves as they came.
By putting the participle phrase at the beginning or end of the sentence, the participle phrase gets special emphasis. These are known as free modifiers. Though it is important to remember that they will always have a clear connection to its subject.
Participle as an Object Complement
In Chapter 3, there are two functions for adjectivals: subject complement in Patterns II and IV and the object complement in Pattern IX.
Sometimes this adjectival slot is filled by an adjective like: The guard does not seem pleasant.
Sometimes the adjectival slot is filled with a prepositional phrase: The guard was at his post all morning.
Also, we could use prepositional phrases as object complements: I consider your smile among the best I've ever seen.
For participles, there can be an extremely subtle difference between the participle as a direct object or an object complement:
The mechanic found the broken bolt.
The mechanic found the bold broken.
To determine if the participle part of the direct object or an object complement, substitute for pronouns:
The mechanic found it.
The mechanic found it broken.
Another way to determine if the sentence has an object complement is to insert to be:
The mechanic found the to be broken bolt. (This doesn't make sense, so the adjectival participle phrase is part of the direct object.)
The mechanic found the bolt to be broken. (This makes sense, which means broken is an object complement here.)
Dangling Participle
A dangling participle is a verb without a subject.
Having seen the new cars at the dealership, the new vehicle was the best choice.
(Here, the "new vehicle" isn't doing the seeing.)
A source of dangling participles is that the sentence has a delayed subject. There are two common reasons for these delayed subjects:
  1. There transformations (Chapter 5)
    • Having left the car on all night, there was no more gas.
  2. Anticipatory it (Chapter 9)
    • Working hard on painting the walls in my office, it was nice of you to bring me some lunch.
To fix this, it does not help to move the dangling participle phrase to the end of the sentence.
The best way to fix delayed subjects to revise the sentence and expand the participle phrase into a complete clause.
After we left the car on all night, there was no more gas.
It was nice of you to bring me some lunch when you knew I was working hard on painting the walls in my office.
Another issue that can cause dangling participles is passive voice.
Having been working on these damn notes for 13 hours, the notes had still not been completed before the final exam.
The problem with the passive voice is that the missing agent for the verb "completed." Adjusting the sentence to be active voice will fix the issue:
Having been working on these damn notes for 13 hours, I still have not completed the notes before the final exam.
Participles as Adverbials or Adjectivals
Some participle phrases have characteristics of both adjectivals and adverbials.
Walking in the blizzard, Pete got really cold.
The opening verb phrase can be expanded into an adjectival or an adverbial.
Pete, who was walking in the blizzard, got really cold. (adjectival participle phrase)
While he was walking in the blizzard, Pete got really cold. (adverbial participle phrase - see Chapter 6)

Punctuation of Clauses and Participles

When figuring out punctuation for clauses and participles, one needs to question if it is a restrictive modifier or nonrestrictive modifier. One needs to be set off by commas; one does not.

Restrictive Modifiers
It might be easier to think of this as a defining or identifying modifier. The question to ask is: Does this clause's information directly identify or define the noun? And is this information required? If it does directly identify or define and is required information, then clause is restrictive and does not need to be set off by commas.
Ex: The teacher wearing a blue shirt is the math teacher.
Here, we assume that by identifying the teacher by wearing a blue shirt that they are the only one wearing a blue shirt. By saying "the teacher wearing a blue shirt," we indicate that we are specifically identifying THAT teacher.
Nonrestrictive Modifiers
For this one, it might be easier to think of these as a commenting modifier. The question to ask is: Does the modifier comment on (not restrict) the noun? Is the information required? If it does merely comments on the noun and is not required to identify the noun, or in other words is simply additional information, then the clause is nonrestrictive and does need to be set off by commas.
My sister, who is turning 22 this month, works at Pizza Hut.
The clause "who is turning 22 this month," is not required information to the sentence and merely comments extra details about my sister, which means that the clause is nonrestrictive and does need to be set off by commas.

Many times, this comes down to context, which is why determining restrictive and nonrestrictive clauses can be tricky or up to the writer to decide, as setting a clause off with commas can change the entire meaning of the sentence.

The power went out during the thunderstorm. All of my family members who were more adventurous than I went outside and got wet. I stayed inside, wrapped up in a blanket.
The power went out during the thunderstorm. All of myy family members, who were more adventurous than I, went outside and got wet. I stayed inside, wrapped up in a blanket.

The first sentence suggests that all of the family members went out side and got wet. Whereas the second sentence suggests that only the family members who were more adventurous went outside and got wet.

Qualifiers

Qualifiers or Intensifiers (267-8)
Positive Qualifiers
These qualifiers can be used with the positive form of most adjectives.
Ex: very cute, quite simple, rather smart, really ugly, fairly recent, too big
Comparative Qualifiers
These can be paired with the comparative degree of adjectives (like newer and sweeter) and with comparative adverbs (like sooner and farther).
Ex: still doing better, no sooner than 8 am, even farther, much more beautiful.
Limited-Distribution Qualifiers
These qualify things to show a status.
Ex: right now, wide awake, just so, just about there, almost there.
Colloquial Expression Qualifiers
These are just expressions to look for.
Ex: darn right, damn sure, real pretty.
Adverbs of Manner as Qualifiers
The -ly adverbs can be used as qualifiers with certain adjectives.
Ex: This is an unusually peculiar feeling. The chemistry exam is extremely difficult.
